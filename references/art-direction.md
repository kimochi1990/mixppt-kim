# Art Direction

## Choose a direction from the context

- Corporate or board: restrained palette, high information clarity, decisive titles, native charts, consistent grid.
- Editorial or thought leadership: expressive typography, generous whitespace, strong image crops, varied but controlled pacing.
- Technical or product: diagrams, architecture views, code or UI details, precise annotations, functional color.
- Academic: evidence-first composition, restrained styling, visible citations, one primary exhibit per result slide.
- Investor pitch: sharp problem-to-opportunity narrative, confident metrics, memorable product moments, credible restraint.
- Keynote: fewer words, larger type, cinematic pacing, one idea or reveal per slide.

Do not force a universal template onto every subject. Derive the visual system from audience, content, brand, and delivery setting.

## Declare the visual system

Before full production, specify:

- Aspect ratio and safe margins.
- Column grid and alignment anchors.
- Display, title, subtitle, body, caption, and source type sizes.
- Font pairing and installed fallbacks.
- Background, foreground, accent, semantic, and muted color roles.
- Spacing rhythm, line weight, corner style, image treatment, and recurring components.
- Chart and table conventions.

Reuse the system consistently. Introduce variation through composition and scale, not arbitrary styling.

## Composition rules

- Give each slide one communicative purpose and one dominant visual hierarchy.
- Use whitespace to separate ideas and create emphasis.
- Keep critical elements aligned to a small set of grid anchors.
- Prefer a few large, meaningful elements to many small decorative ones.
- Repeat components for coherence, then vary scale or placement to maintain rhythm.
- Use real content in sample slides; placeholder text hides density and hierarchy problems.

## Typography

- Size body copy for presentation distance, not laptop reading.
- Keep line lengths comfortable and avoid long centered paragraphs.
- Use no more font families than the concept requires; two is usually sufficient.
- Use weight, scale, spacing, and position before adding extra colors or effects.
- Never shrink text simply to rescue an overloaded slide. Edit or split the content.

## Color and accessibility

- Maintain sufficient contrast for text, labels, and essential graphical marks.
- Do not encode meaning through color alone; pair it with labels, shapes, or patterns.
- Reserve saturated accent colors for emphasis and data meaning.
- Test charts and key slides in grayscale when color distinctions are important.

## Imagery

- Use images that add evidence, context, emotion, or explanation.
- Crop decisively and protect focal points from text overlays.
- Keep image treatment consistent across the deck.
- Do not use decorative AI imagery as fake evidence, a product screenshot, a research figure, or a factual scene.
- Prefer native diagrams and shapes when relationships matter more than atmosphere.

## Charts and tables

- Choose the simplest chart that supports the intended comparison.
- Emphasize the conclusion with direct labels and selective color.
- Remove nonessential gridlines, legends, borders, and redundant decimals.
- Keep scales honest and units explicit.
- Convert dense tables into prioritized comparisons or split them across slides.

## Avoid

- Gratuitous gradients, glass effects, floating cards, and decorative dashboards.
- Tiny text, excessive footnotes, and dense bullet walls.
- Random icon styles, inconsistent corner radii, and unmotivated shadows.
- Generic stock-photo montages unrelated to the argument.
- Flattening an editable deck into full-slide images unless the user explicitly chooses that tradeoff.
