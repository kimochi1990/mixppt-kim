# Storytelling and Evidence

## Frame the communication

Identify the audience, what they know, what they should believe or decide afterward, and the time available. Build for that communication job rather than merely summarizing the source.

## Build the story spine

Use the simplest fitting structure:

- Situation → complication → resolution for decisions and strategy.
- Question → evidence → answer for analysis and research.
- Problem → insight → product → proof → ask for pitches.
- Context → method → findings → implications for academic work.

Apply pyramid structure: lead with the governing idea, then support it with grouped evidence.

## Action titles

Write titles as complete claims or takeaways when evidence permits. “Revenue grew because retention improved” is more useful than “Revenue.” Avoid overstating uncertain evidence.

Run the title-only story test: read every slide title in order. The sequence should communicate a coherent argument without body copy. Rewrite or reorder until it does.

## Slide purpose and evidence

- Give every slide one communicative purpose.
- Use one primary exhibit or visual argument on evidence-heavy slides.
- Annotate the important finding near the evidence.
- Separate facts, interpretation, and recommendation.
- Remove content that does not advance the story; report material cuts when redesigning user files.
- Split slides when two independent conclusions compete for attention.

## Openings and closings

Open with relevance: the decision, tension, opportunity, or question. Avoid agenda slides unless navigation genuinely helps the audience.

Close with synthesis and a clear next step, decision, implication, or memorable final idea. Do not end on a generic “Thank you” slide when a useful conclusion can remain on screen.

## Citations

- Cite borrowed data, figures, images, and substantive claims close to where they appear.
- Keep citations readable and consistent.
- Include a references slide when the context requires it, especially for academic and analytical decks.
- Never invent a source, author, date, metric, or URL.

## Speaker notes

Use notes for nuance, transitions, caveats, definitions, and delivery cues that would overload the slide. Notes should extend the visual story, not duplicate all visible text.

## Density reduction order

When a slide is too dense:

1. Remove repetition and nonessential qualifiers.
2. Rewrite prose as a precise claim plus evidence.
3. Move nuance to speaker notes.
4. Replace lists with a diagram or prioritized comparison when the relationship matters.
5. Split the slide if it still contains multiple purposes.

Do not use unreadably small type as a density-management strategy.
